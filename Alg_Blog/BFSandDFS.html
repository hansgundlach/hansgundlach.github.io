Graph Searching

BFS and DFS

Motivation

Grap searching is very important topic in computer science. Basic algorithms like Breadth First Search form the foundation of most graph algorithms including shortest path and minimum spanning tree algorithms. Pratcily graph searching is useful in network anlaysis ie any system with two or more interacting agents. This includes a lot of things in fact it includes almost eveything.

Breadth First Search

Picture of Breadth First Search taken from wikimedia

For a graph and a source vertex , breadth-first search traverses the edges of to find all reachable vertices from . It also computes the shortest distance to any reachable vertex. Any path between two points in a breadth-first search tree corresponds to the shortest path from the root to any other node . There are three types of vertices in BFS: tree vertices, those that have been visited; fringe vertices, those adjacent to tree vertices but not yet visited; and undiscovered vertices, those that we have not encountered yet. In the animation above, white indicates vertices that are undiscovered, grey indicates fringe vertices, and black indicates tree vertices. To systematically search a connected component of a graph, begin with one vertex on the fringe, all others unseen, and perform the following step until all vertices have been visited: "move one vertex from the fringe to the tree, and put any unseen vertices adjacent to on the fringe. " Graph traversal methods differ in how it is decided which vertex should be moved from the fringe to the tree. For breadth-first search, choose the vertex from the fringe that was least recently encountered; this corresponds using a queue to hold vertices on the fringe.

Depth First Search

The strategy of depth-first search is to explore deeper into the graph whenever possible. Depth-first search explores edges that come out of the most recently discovered vertex, . Only edges going to unexplored vertices are explored. When all of edges have been explored, the search backtracks until it reaches an unexplored neighbor. This process continues until all of the vertices that are reachable from the original source vertex are discovered. If there are any unvisited vertices, depth-ﬁrst search picks one of them as a new source and repeats the search from that vertex. The algorithm repeats this process until it has discovered every vertex. This algorithm is careful not to repeat vertices, so each vertex is explored once. DFS uses a stack data structure to keep track of vertices​.

Practice and Details

Depending on your graph type and problem BFS or DFS may be better. Both DFS and BFS have $O( V + E )$ complexity. DFS acourse is more useful when you believe that your chosen node is farther down on a graph. If your search tree is impractilcy wide you may choose to use DFs over BFS to better allocate memory. For very large graphs that can not be stored automaticly BFS's complexity for finding a node d branches away is $O(b^d)$ where b is the branching factor.
